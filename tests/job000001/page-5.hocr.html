4. Discussions

Although the parent plant is unknown, S phinxiocarpon wuha- nium has been described as an enclosed seed—like structure, most possibly belonging to the class Lycopsida (Xue, 2007, 2010). In an overwhelming majority of specimens, S. wuhcmium is individ- ually preserved, as either a sporangium—sporophyll complex or a detached megasporangium. Occasionally, S. wuhanium shows the parallel and overlapping nature of two or three adjacent indi- viduals or appears to have been aggregated into a strobilus—like organ (Li et al., 1997; Xue, 2007, 2010).

S. wuhanium has parallel striations on the surface in acute

angles along the greater diameter (Xue, 2007, 2010; this study). Probably, these striations represent ﬁbers within the sporangial wall and sporophyll tissue. Remarkably, those ﬁbers within the sporophyll tissue appear to form an acute angle with the longer axis of the seed—like organ and serve as a structural mechanic to dehisce when S. wuhanium becomes mature. Such a dehis- cence mechanism is very similar to the extant angiosperm fruits (Roth, 1977), for example, the explosively opening and twisted valves of mature pods in Wisteria sinensis (Sims) Sweet and W ﬂoribunda (Willd.) DC. (Zhang and Wang, 2010). The curling, inverted “S—shaped” morphology of sporophylls in the present specimens of S. wuhanium, to some extent, lends credence to our hypothesis. Hence, the indehiscent specimens of S. wuhcmium in previous reports are likely to be immature or in the develop- mental mid—late stages whereas the dehiscent specimens in this study may be mature or in the ﬁnal developmental stage.

Each reproductive unit of S. wuhanium consists of an enclosed sporangium—sporophyll complex that is tardily open, reﬂecting a kind of contingent dehiscence mechanism. The pol- lination and fertilization of S. wuhanium may not occur until the enclosed sporangium—sporophyll complex as a more likely dis- seminule is detached from the strobilus—like reproductive organ of the parent plant, transported some distances by the media (e.g., wind and/or water), and becomes open to meet with the microspores. Hence, the inferred life history of S. wuhcmium may be similar to some Carboniferous arborescent lycopsids, e.g., Diaphorodendron DiMichele, whose reproduction is char- acterized by separate megasporangiate and microsporangiate strobili, and may achieve fertilization in the water by the meeting of dispersed microspores and detached sporangium—sporophyll complexes with single functional megaspore (Phillips and DiMichele, 1992). Such a pollination mechanism may avoid

an excessive inbreeding in an individual parent plant or popu- lation (Husband and Schemske, 1996; Charlesworth and Willis,

2009).

S. wuhcmium only occurred in the early Late Devonian of Hubei, China and might represent an abnormal developmen- tal form by genetic mutation and/or ecological adaptation to the local adversity (e.g., aridity, torridity, ﬂood, wind, shady habitat,

and insect attack). As land plants in the Devonian became larger and larger and thereby formed the early forest ecosys- tems casting dense shade, selective pressure may increasingly favour a k—strategy (sensu Harper, 1977) with larger dis seminules (e.g., megaspores, preovules and seeds) (Chaloner and Sheerin, 1981). Such developmental mutation and ecological adapta- tion are, more or less, parallel to the evolution of the seed habit in seed plants, which has been canalized by some biotic or abiotic selective pressures (DiMichele et al., 1989; Haig and Westoby, 1989; Herr, 1995; Rowe, 1997; Prestianni and Gerrienne, 2010).
