New materials of Sphinxiocarpon, a seed—like organ of putative lycopsid afﬁnity, from the Late Devonian of Hubei, China

Abstract

New materials of a seed—like organ, Sphinxiocarpon wuhanium (Li, Hilton et Hemsley) Wang, Xue et Prestianni, from the lower part of the Wutung Formation (Frasnian) at Xianniishan Quarry of Wuhan, eastern Hubei, China provide new insights into its dehiscence mechanism. It is suggested that the oblique, longitudinally striated ﬁbers within the sporophyll tissue serve as a structural mechanic to dehisce, and that the ﬁmbriately opened apex of S. wuhanium is likely a result of the tissular dissolution and mechanical dynamics within its sporophyll and sporangial wall when it becomes mature. Sphinxiocarpon Wang, Xue et Prestianni, neither a seed nor a preovule, has the closest afﬁnity to lycopsids and

might represent an aberrant developmental form and/or an ecological adaptation to the local adversity. © 2012 Elsevier B.V. and Nanjing Institute of Geology and Palaeontology, CAS. All rights reserved.

Keywords: Dehiscence mechanism; Late Devonian; Lycopsid; Preovule; Seed; Sphinxiocarpon

1. Introduction

Sphinxiocarpon Wang, Xue et Prestianni (=Sphinxia Li, Hilton et Hemsley), with only one species S. wuhanium (Li, Hilton et Hemsley) Wang, Xue et Prestianni, was described as a seed—like structure from the early Late Devonian of Mil- iangshan Quarry of Wuhan, eastem Hubei, China (Li et al., 1997; Wang et al., 2007, 2008). Additional specimens of S. wuhanium were reported from the coeval Huangjiadeng For- mation of Changyang, western Hubei (Xue, 2007, 2010). S. wuhanium has been thought to lack a direct afﬁnity to the pro gymnosperm—spermatophyte lineage of the early land plants. Therefore, Sphinxiocarpon is neither a seed nor a preovule. Xue (2007, 2010) assumed that Sphinxiocarpon might have a very close afﬁnity to the class Lycopsida, representing a unique clade that is different from any other known heterosporous lycopsids. However, many aspects remain unknown about the evolutionary biology of Sphinxiocarpon. This short communi- cation is to describe some newly discovered specimens showing the dehiscent morphology of Sphinxiocarpon and to discuss its dehiscence (accordingly pollination) mechanism.

2. Materials and methods

All plant fossils in this study were discovered from the lower part of the Wutung Formation at Xianniishan Quarry (GPS: 30° 34’ 28.9’ ’ N, 1 14°10’ 18.4”E) near Wuhan City, eastern Hubei, China (Fig. 1A, B); ten pieces of specimens (compressions and impressions) were collected from a 2.4—m thick bed (i.e., the fourth bed, see Fig. 2) in greyish white mudstone and claystone. Abundant lycopsids, including S ublepidodendron grabaui (Sze) Wang et Xu, S. songziense Chen ex Wang et al., Lepidodendrop— sis hirmeri Lutz, and Leptophloeum rhombicum Dawson, were found from the same bed as well as the overlying ﬁfth and tenth beds (Fig. 2).

The Wutung Formation in the whole Yangtze Valley of South China is diachronous (Li et al., 1984; Zhang et al., 1987; Xia, 1992), ranging in age from early Late Devonian to early Early Carboniferous. In the Lower Yangtze Valley, the Wutung For- mation is late Late Devonian and early Carboniferous (Lu, 1994; Wang, 2002; Wang et al., 2002, 2003, 2012; Hou and Q1, 2006).
